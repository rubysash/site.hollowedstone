The Inner Work

A game of balance and lifelong growth for two players — Playtest draft
The privilege of a lifetime is to become who you truly are. Carl Jung

The Inner Work is a game about the four capacities every person carries and the lifelong effort to keep them in balance. Two sides of the same self compete for dominance. No single capacity makes a whole person. The one who advances all four in formation toward the center wins — not by defeating the other side, but by doing the work long enough and honestly enough to hold the middle ground.

Quick Rules — Read This First
1
Four capacities, each balancing one other. Courage, Planning, Compassion, Discipline. No single capacity is enough alone. Each one, taken too far without the others, becomes the thing that holds you back. You carry two. Your opponent carries the other two.
2
Setup. Each player places 5 stones, 3 of one capacity and 2 of the other, across their starting edge in any order. That choice is your first act of self-awareness.
3
Every turn you get 2 moves. One stone, one step each. Both moves must follow the partnership rule: a stone may only advance toward center if it starts adjacent to a friendly stone. No capacity advances alone.
4
Displace by moving onto an enemy hex your capacity balances. That stone is not gone. Your opponent may spend one of their moves to restore it. Nothing is permanently lost. Every capacity can be recovered.
5
The Inner Work is the center hex. Reaching it means all your capacities are operating together in the open. You are committed. You cannot leave voluntarily. While you hold it you get 3 moves per turn. It is the hardest place to stay.
6
When your opponent holds the center your partnership rule is suspended. One side of you loses its ordered direction. You get 2 free moves and 1 may be a displacement, aimed at restoring balance and reclaiming the center.
7
First to 5 points wins. The work is never finished. But the person who keeps moving toward the center shapes who they become. The one who waits lets imbalance decide for them.

The Four Capacities

Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response. In our response lies our growth and our freedom. Viktor Frankl
You do not have to be good. You do not have to walk on your knees for a hundred miles through the desert repenting. You only have to let the soft animal of your body love what it loves. Mary Oliver

Each capacity is a genuine strength. Each one, without the others to balance it, becomes its own kind of prison. The goal is not to eliminate any of them. The goal is to keep all four moving forward together.

Courage (Red) Acts when action is needed. Breaks through fear and inertia. Moves before the conditions are perfect. In excess: recklessness. Acts without thinking. Burns bridges. Mistakes speed for progress.
Planning (Blue) Prepares the ground before advancing. Sees the board several moves ahead. Protects what matters. In excess: paralysis. Prepares forever. Waits for conditions that never arrive. Mistakes thinking for doing.
Compassion (Green) Holds others and self with patience. Restores what discipline has broken. Knows when to soften. In excess: codependency. Loses self in others. Cannot hold a boundary. Gives until empty.
Discipline (Gold) Builds the habits that outlast motivation. Shows up when feeling does not. Turns intention into structure. In excess: rigidity. Cannot adapt. Turns self-improvement into self-punishment. Mistakes control for strength.

The Balance Cycle

Knowing yourself is the beginning of all wisdom. Aristotle

Each capacity balances the excess of one other. No capacity is the villain. Each arrow in this cycle is a real truth about how people grow.

Courage balances Planning
Planning balances Compassion
Compassion balances Discipline
Discipline balances Courage

Courage breaks the paralysis of over-planning. Planning rescues the person whose compassion has left them without any structure or boundaries. Compassion softens the person who has turned discipline into self-punishment. Discipline channels the recklessness of unchecked courage into something sustainable.

Player 1 Courage (Red)  &  Compassion (Green)
Player 2 Planning (Blue)  &  Discipline (Gold)

Your two capacities do not balance each other in the cycle. The conflict is always between the two sides of yourself, never within them.

Setup

An unexamined life is not worth living. Socrates
  • Each player has 5 stones, 3 of one capacity and 2 of the other.
  • Place them in any order across your 5-hex starting edge. Your opponent can see your arrangement.
  • Grouping one capacity creates a concentrated front. Alternating spreads your influence and is harder to read. This is your first act of self-awareness before the work begins.
  • Player 2 makes one free move before Player 1 takes their first turn to balance going second.

Your Turn

Players alternate turns. On your turn you get 2 moves. Each move is one stone, one step, to any adjacent empty hex. While you hold the center you get 3 moves instead of 2.

If you have no legal moves you must pass. When a person stops moving toward growth, imbalance fills the space they left.

The Partnership Rule

You cannot be lonely if you like the person you are alone with. Wayne Dyer
No man is an island, entire of itself. Every man is a piece of the continent, a part of the main. John Donne
Growth is not meant to be done alone. The capacities we neglect most are usually the ones we most need a partner to model for us. Brene Brown

A stone may only advance toward center if it begins its move adjacent to at least one other friendly stone. Isolated stones may only move sideways or away from center.

No capacity grows in isolation. Courage without any companion becomes recklessness before it reaches the center. The two capacities you carry must advance together or they do not advance at all.

The partnership rule is suspended when your opponent holds the center. The reason is explained below.

Displacement and Restoration

You may encounter many defeats, but you must not be defeated. In fact, it may be necessary to encounter the defeats so you can know who you are, what you can rise from, how you can still come out of it. Maya Angelou
Fall seven times, stand up eight. Japanese proverb

Move your stone onto a hex occupied by an enemy stone your capacity balances. Your stone takes the hex. The displaced stone goes to your opponent's holding area.

No capacity is permanently lost. When your stone is displaced it goes to holding. On any of your turns you may spend one of your 2 moves to restore it to any empty hex on your starting edge. The capacity returns. It is humbled, reoriented, and back at the starting point. But it is not gone. It never was.

Forcing your opponent to spend moves restoring instead of advancing is a strategy in itself. A person who spends all their energy recovering from setbacks cannot press forward. Sustained pressure is often more powerful than a single decisive displacement.

Ko Rule

A stone may not return to a hex it occupied two or fewer moves ago. Growth does not come from retreating to the same position repeatedly.

The Inner Work — Center Hex

The most terrifying thing is to accept oneself completely. Carl Jung
Owning our story and loving ourselves through that process is the bravest thing we will ever do. Brene Brown
To know what you prefer instead of humbly saying Amen to what the world tells you you ought to prefer, is to have kept your soul alive. Robert Louis Stevenson

The center hex is The Inner Work. Arriving there means all your capacities are operating together in the open. You are not hiding any part of yourself. You are integrated, visible, and fully committed to the work. That is the hardest place to be and the most worthwhile.

Anyone can claim the center for a time. Imbalance can occupy the middle of a life for a season. But the opposition it draws will displace it eventually. The center does not hold for the person who stands there for the wrong reasons.

Claiming the center: Move onto the center hex on your turn. That stone is now committed. It cannot leave voluntarily. It can only be displaced by your opponent. Your extra third move applies to your other stones, not to the one holding the center. Holding the inner work requires the rest of your capacities to keep advancing behind it.
The center is open to every capacity. The stone holding the center may displace any adjacent enemy regardless of the normal cycle. Any adjacent enemy of any capacity may also displace it regardless of the cycle. When you are fully open and integrated, every part of yourself can reach you. That is the vulnerability and the strength of doing the inner work in the open.

When the Opponent Holds the Center

The curious paradox is that when I accept myself just as I am, then I can change. Carl Rogers
We cannot solve our problems with the same thinking we used when we created them. Albert Einstein

When the opposing side of yourself holds the center, your normal formation breaks down. One dominant capacity has claimed the whole of your attention and the others have lost their ordered direction. In these moments a single focused capacity must move with urgency toward restoring balance, even without its partner alongside it.

This is not abandoning the work. This is the crisis response that growth sometimes requires. One part of you breaks from the normal pattern with a specific purpose: to dislodge the imbalance and restore the conditions that allow all four capacities to advance together again.

The Restoration Turn: When your opponent holds the center your partnership rule is fully suspended. You get 2 moves. One of those 2 moves may be a displacement. Send a single capacity directly toward the center to challenge the imbalance. Or use it to displace an isolated enemy stone and cut their partnership before they can restore formation. You may not make 2 displacements in the same turn. One act of restoration. Choose what the moment actually needs.

Winning — The Ongoing Work

What you are is God's gift to you. What you become is your gift to God. Hans Urs von Balthasar
We are not human beings having a spiritual experience. We are spiritual beings having a human experience. Pierre Teilhard de Chardin
It is not the mountain we conquer, but ourselves. Edmund Hillary

First player to reach 5 points wins immediately. Every displacement scores 1 point regardless of location.

The player who acts with all four capacities working in formation will shape the board. The player who lets one capacity dominate while the others stall will find the center always just out of reach. The inner work is not about being perfect. It is about keeping all four moving forward together for long enough to hold the ground that matters.

Tiebreaker

If both players reach 5 in the same turn, the player with the higher total count of displacements wins. If equal, the player who scored their fifth point later in the turn wins. If simultaneous, Player 2 wins.

Notes on Growth

The wound is the place where the light enters you. Rumi
  • Your opening arrangement is a self-portrait. How you place your capacities before the work begins tells you something about which ones you trust most and which ones you keep at a distance. Pay attention to that.
  • Partnership is not weakness. Two capacities advancing together cover more ground than one pressing forward alone. The person who lets all four parts of themselves work in coordination advances further and holds ground longer.
  • Displacement is not failure. When a capacity is pushed back it returns. It is humbled and reoriented, not destroyed. The work is in how quickly you restore it and return it to the journey.
  • The center demands everything. Do not claim the inner work without your other capacities arriving behind you. A person who stands fully open without any supporting work around them is one moment of opposition away from being displaced entirely.
  • The restoration turn is a gift, not a crisis. When your opponent dominates the center, your normal patterns break open. That is an opportunity. A single focused capacity can cut through the imbalance and change the shape of the whole board. Use it with intention.
  • Know the cycle before every move. Before moving any stone, know what it balances and what balances it. Advancing a capacity into a position where it is immediately displaced is one of the most common ways the work stalls. Look one move ahead. Then two.

The Simple Version

In the middle of every difficulty lies opportunity. Albert Einstein

Everyone has four things inside them. Courage to act. Planning to prepare. Compassion to be kind, especially to yourself. Discipline to keep going when you do not feel like it.

Too much of any one of them causes a different kind of trouble. Too much courage becomes reckless. Too much planning becomes stuck. Too much compassion loses itself. Too much discipline becomes cruel.

They need each other. You cannot advance one without the others moving alongside it. Nobody grows alone.

The middle of the board is the hardest place to be. It means all four parts of you are working together in the open at the same time. You cannot pretend there. You cannot hide. And you cannot leave until something pushes you out.

When that happens you come back. You always come back. No part of you is ever gone for good.

The person who keeps moving toward the center shapes who they become. The person who waits for the right moment just lets imbalance decide instead.

Playtest Variables

  • 5-point target: try 4 if games run long, try 6 if games end too quickly
  • 3 moves while holding the center: try 2 if the center position feels too dominant
  • Restoration turn displacement limit of 1: try 2 if dislodging the center feels too difficult
  • Partnership rule: relax to "within 2 steps of a friendly" if the opening feels too slow
  • Player 2 free move: remove if Player 2 wins too consistently despite it
  • Ko rule: extend to 3 moves if stalling persists
