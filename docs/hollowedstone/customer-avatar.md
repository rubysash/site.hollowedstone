# Customer Avatar

Who pays for abstract strategy board games, and what do they actually spend?

---

## The Core Paying Customer

### Demographics

**Age:** 30-55. Skews male (70/30) but the female segment is growing, especially in couples who play together. The sweet spot is 35-45: old enough to have disposable income, young enough to be comfortable playing online.

**Income:** $40,000-$90,000 household. Not wealthy. These are teachers, IT workers, librarians, engineers, hobbyist woodworkers, and retirees. They are price-sensitive but will spend on things they value. They are NOT the same audience as the $200 Kickstarter boardgame crowd.

**Location:** US, UK, Canada, Australia, Germany, Netherlands, Scandinavia. English-speaking or European. Strong overlap with countries where traditional board gaming culture exists.

**Education:** Above average. Abstract strategy games attract analytical thinkers. Many have college degrees or trade skills. They read. They research before buying.

**Tech comfort:** Moderate. They can use a browser and find a game link, but they are not power users. They will not install an app or create an account unless the value is obvious. They are more likely to find you through a Google search for "play Tablut online" than through social media.

---

### Psychographics

**What they value:**
- Intellectual challenge without randomness (no dice, no luck)
- History and cultural depth behind the games
- Simplicity of rules with depth of play
- Physical craftsmanship (wood, stone, handmade objects)
- Quiet, screen-optional hobbies they can share with one other person
- Not being marketed to aggressively

**What they do NOT value:**
- Flashy graphics or animations
- Social features, chat, or community drama
- Competitive rankings or ELO systems
- Subscription models or recurring charges
- Anything that feels like a mobile game or gacha mechanic

**Buying behavior:**
- They research before purchasing. They will read the rules page and play 3-5 games before considering a purchase.
- They spend $10-30 on a board game, not $60-150. They shop at thrift stores, craft fairs, and Etsy, not specialty game shops.
- They respond to "handmade by one person" better than "premium brand."
- They are more likely to buy a gift for someone else than for themselves.
- They will pay for a PDF only if it looks good enough to frame or if it includes real strategy content, not just the rules.
- Impulse purchases are rare. The buying cycle is days to weeks, not minutes.

**Where they spend time online:**
- BoardGameGeek forums (browsing, not posting)
- Reddit: r/boardgames, r/abstractgames, r/chess (lurking)
- YouTube: board game review channels (Shut Up & Sit Down, No Pun Included, but they also watch smaller channels)
- Etsy: searching for handmade game sets
- Google: searching for rules and history of specific games
- They are NOT on TikTok, Instagram, or Twitter for board game content

---

## The Free User (and why they still matter)

The vast majority of visitors will never pay. But they serve two purposes:

1. **They are the opponent.** Every paying customer needs someone to play against. Free users fill the other seat. If nobody is online to accept a game code, the paying customer leaves too.

2. **They are the funnel.** A small percentage of free users convert to email signups, and a smaller percentage of those convert to buyers. At 10k games/month, you might get 200 email signups, 10 PDF purchases, and 3-5 physical set orders. The free users are the top of that funnel.

**Cost to serve free users:** Near zero. Cloudflare free tier handles the traffic. No accounts, no storage per user, no support burden. The cost of a free user is approximately $0.00. Keep them.

## Realistic Price Sensitivity

Based on who this customer actually is:

| Product | What they expect to pay | What feels too expensive |
|---------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Basic PDF (rules + board printout) | Free or $1-2 | $5+ |
| Premium PDF (strategy guide, multiple layouts, nice art) | $3-5 | $8+ |
| 3D printed piece set (no board) | $8-15 | $25+ |
| CNC/laser cut board only | $15-25 | $40+ |
| Complete set (board + pieces + rules card) | $20-35 | $50+ |
| Premium hardwood set | $40-70 | $100+ |
| Newsletter | Free (ad-supported) | Any cost |
| Tournament entry | $3-5 | $10+ |
| Ad-free experience | $2-3/year | $5+/year |

These are lower than my earlier estimates. The abstract strategy game audience is niche and frugal. They will pay for craftsmanship, but they compare against mass-produced chess sets ($15 at Walmart) and free printable games. Your pricing needs to anchor against those, not against boutique board game Kickstarters.

## The One Customer to Optimize For

**Name:** "David" (or "Karen" - the gift buyer variant)

David is 42. He works in IT. He played chess as a kid but got bored of it. He discovered abstract strategy games through a YouTube video about Go and fell down the rabbit hole. He searched "play Tablut online free" and found Hollowed Stone. He played 8 games of Tablut over two weeks against a friend. He noticed the "Rulebooks" link, read the Tablut history, and thought "this would be cool to have as a physical board." He saw the link to buy a set. He spent $28 on a CNC-cut Tablut board with 3D printed pieces. He told his friend about it. His friend bought one too.

David will never create an account. He will never join a Discord. He will never subscribe to a newsletter unless you give him something worth printing. He responds to craftsmanship, history, and the fact that this was made by one person, not a corporation. He is your customer.

Karen is 48. She is buying a gift for her husband who "likes strategy games but already has too many chess sets." She searched "unique strategy board games handmade" and found your Etsy shop. She bought a Surakarta set ($30) because it looked beautiful and she had never heard of it. She does not play on the website. She is 40% of your Etsy revenue.

## What This Means for the Platform

1. **Keep it free to play.** The game itself is the marketing. Never gate gameplay behind payment.
2. **The website is a showroom.** Its job is to let people fall in love with a game, then offer them the physical version.
3. **Price below the "impulse buy" threshold** for the primary products ($20-35 for physical sets, $3-5 for PDFs).
4. **The email list is the long game.** It takes 6-12 months to build, but it is the only channel you fully own.
5. **Do not build features for free users** (accounts, profiles, rankings, social). Build features that convert free users to email subscribers and email subscribers to buyers.
6. **The craft is the brand.** One person making handmade game sets from historical designs is a story that sells itself. Do not try to look like a company. Look like a craftsperson.
